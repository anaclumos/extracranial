> In [internationalization](./../.././docs/pages/Internationalization.md), CJK character is a collective term for the [Chinese](./../.././docs/pages/%EC%A4%91%EA%B5%AD%EC%96%B4.md), [Japanese](./../.././docs/pages/%EC%9D%BC%EB%B3%B8%EC%96%B4.md), and [Korean](./../.././docs/pages/%ED%95%9C%EA%B5%AD%EC%96%B4.md) languages, which include [Chinese characters](./../.././docs/pages/%ED%95%9C%EC%9E%90.md) and derivatives in their writing systems, sometimes paired with other scripts. The CJK characters often have [Hànzì](./../.././docs/pages/%ED%95%9C%EC%9E%90.md) in [Chinese](./../.././docs/pages/%EC%A4%91%EA%B5%AD%EC%96%B4.md), [Kanji](./../.././docs/pages/%ED%95%9C%EC%9E%90.md), and Kana in [Japanese](./../.././docs/pages/%EC%9D%BC%EB%B3%B8%EC%96%B4.md) and Hanja and Hangul in [Korean](./../.././docs/pages/%ED%95%9C%EA%B5%AD%EC%96%B4.md). Vietnamese can be included, making the abbreviation CJKV, as Vietnamese historically used [Chinese characters](./../.././docs/pages/%ED%95%9C%EC%9E%90.md) which they were known as Chữ Hán and Chữ Nôm in Vietnamese (Hán-Nôm altogether).
